# **1\. Background**

Food–drug interactions (FDIs) can significantly alter the **safety, efficacy, and pharmacokinetics of medications**. These interactions arise through multiple mechanisms, including:

• inhibition or induction of metabolic enzymes (e.g., CYP pathways)  
 • modulation of drug transporters (e.g., OATP, LAT1)  
 • nutrient–drug chelation affecting absorption  
 • pharmacodynamic antagonism or potentiation  
 • changes in gastrointestinal physiology affecting bioavailability

Well-known examples include:

* grapefruit juice increasing exposure of CYP3A substrates

* dietary protein competing with levodopa transport across the blood–brain barrier

* calcium and iron reducing absorption of tetracyclines and fluoroquinolones

* vitamin K intake influencing warfarin anticoagulation

Despite their clinical importance, food–drug interaction knowledge remains **fragmented across regulatory documents**, clinical literature, and pharmacology resources.

A major authoritative source of interaction information is **regulatory drug labeling**.

In the United States, prescription drug labels are published in **Structured Product Labeling (SPL)** format and made available through repositories such as DailyMed. These labels contain information on:

• food effects on pharmacokinetics  
 • clinically significant interactions  
 • timing guidance regarding food intake  
 • mechanistic explanations of interactions

However, these data are written primarily as **narrative text**, which limits computational accessibility and integration into digital health systems.

---

# **2\. Problem Statement**

Although regulatory drug labels contain high-value interaction information, they present several challenges:

1. **Narrative structure**  
    Food–drug interactions are described in free text rather than structured data.

2. **Sectional fragmentation**  
    Interaction information may appear in multiple label sections, including:

* Clinical Pharmacology

* Drug Interactions

* Dosage and Administration

* Warnings and Precautions

3. **Inconsistent wording**  
    Similar interactions may be described differently across labels.

4. **Limited machine-readability**  
    Existing drug interaction databases rarely incorporate food interactions systematically or with explicit provenance.

As a result, it remains difficult to build **computational systems that reliably extract and analyze food–drug interaction knowledge at scale**.

---

# **3\. Research Objective**

This project aims to develop an **AI-assisted system that extracts food–drug interaction claims from regulatory drug labeling and converts them into a structured, provenance-linked dataset**.

The resulting dataset will provide a **machine-readable knowledge base of food–drug interactions derived directly from authoritative regulatory sources**.

The system will prioritize:

• provenance and traceability to label text  
 • evidence classification  
 • structured representation of interaction mechanisms

---

# **4\. System Overview**

The proposed system consists of four primary components.

### **1\. Label Ingestion**

The system will ingest regulatory drug labeling from publicly available sources, including:

• U.S. FDA Structured Product Labeling (SPL)  
 • DailyMed label archives  
 • European electronic product information (ePI)

Label XML documents will be parsed to extract relevant sections.

---

### **2\. Interaction Extraction**

Large language models and rule-based extraction methods will identify candidate food–drug interaction statements.

Extraction will target patterns such as:

* “take with food”

* “take on an empty stomach”

* “avoid grapefruit juice”

* “administer at least 2 hours apart from…”

The system will detect and normalize:

• food items or nutrients  
 • affected drugs or drug classes  
 • direction of effect (increase or decrease in exposure)  
 • timing instructions

---

### **3\. Structured Interaction Schema**

Extracted interactions will be represented using a structured schema including:

* drug

* food or nutrient

* interaction type (PK or PD)

* mechanism (when available)

* direction of effect

* timing guidance

* evidence tier

Evidence tiers will classify whether a statement is:

• explicitly stated in regulatory labeling  
 • supported by human clinical studies  
 • mechanistic or in vitro evidence

---

### **4\. Provenance Tracking**

A core feature of the system is **provenance preservation**.

Each extracted claim will include:

* label identifier

* label version and date

* section of the label

* character offsets in the original text

* source snippet

This approach ensures that all outputs remain **traceable to authoritative sources**.
